The Enigma of the Steel Dance: A Comprehensive Analysis of Moreška and the Institutional Stewardship of HGD Sveta Cecilija
The cultural landscape of the eastern Adriatic is defined by a series of high-intensity ritual performances that serve as repositories of historical memory, martial skill, and communal identity. Among these, the Moreška of Korčula stands as the most structurally complex and historically significant example of the "Moros y Cristianos" tradition in modern Europe. While similar sword dances once spanned the Mediterranean, from the Iberian Peninsula to the Italian city-states and the Greek islands, the version preserved in the fortified town of Korčula has evolved into a unique syncretic event that combines blank verse drama, complex orchestration, and physically grueling double-sword choreography. Central to the modern continuity of this performance is the Hrvatsko glazbeno društvo (HGD) Sveta Cecilija, an organization founded in 1883 that has functioned as a cultural bulwark through four distinct political regimes. The following report provides an exhaustive investigation into the historical evolution, narrative architecture, and artistic components of the Moreška, alongside a detailed history of the institutional efforts required to safeguard this protected intangible heritage.   

The Mediterranean Genealogy and Historical Trajectory
The Moreška is fundamentally a "dance of conquest," a stylized representation of the existential conflict between Christian Europe and the Islamic world that dominated the medieval consciousness. Its origins are deeply rooted in the Spanish Reconquista, with the earliest documented performance occurring in Lérida, Spain, in 1150. This initial iteration commemorated the Christian capture of Aragon and the expulsion of the Moors, establishing a performative template of mock battle that would eventually permeate the Mediterranean basin.   

Migration and Localization in the Adriatic
The arrival of the Moreška in the Adriatic is generally dated to the 16th century, though some scholars suggest its influence reached the region as early as the 13th century, evidenced by reports of sword dances in Trogir in 1273. The mechanism of transmission was primarily maritime; trade routes linked Korčula to Sicily, Southern Italy, and Spain, while the town’s status as a Venetian outpost (from 1420 to 1797) facilitated the import of Italian and Spanish cultural forms. By the mid-17th century, the Moreška was firmly integrated into the social fabric of Korčula. The first unambiguous written record of its performance in the town appears in a journal entry dated March 7, 1666, describing a carnival event.   

Historical records indicate that the Moreška was once prevalent across the Dalmatian coast. In Split, a major performance occurred in September 1571 to celebrate the victory over the Ottomans at the Battle of Lepanto, featuring a "race of barbarians" and a staged battle between Ottomans and Moors. However, as the geopolitical landscape shifted and the direct threat of Ottoman or Moorish invasion receded, these dances disappeared from most coastal towns, leaving Korčula as the final bastion of the authentic war-dance form.   

Academic Theories of Survival
The question of why the Moreška survived exclusively in Korčula has long fascinated ethnochoreologists and historians. Several academic theories provide a multifaceted explanation for this cultural anomaly:

The Martial Preparation Hypothesis: Many scholars argue that the Moreška served a practical functional purpose for the town's small island garrison. In an era where Korčula was frequently targeted by pirates and Ottoman raiders, the dance functioned as an intensive "keep-fit" class and sword practice, ensuring that the local male population remained physically capable and skilled in the use of heavy iron blades.   

The "Hidden Transcript" of Resistance: Anthropological research by scholars such as Max Harris and Lada Čale Feldman suggests that the Moreška allowed the local population to manifest a "hidden transcript" of resistance against external rule. While the "public transcript" was a victory of Christians over Moors, the ritualized combat allowed Korčulans to metaphorically express defiance against their Venetian dukes or other governing powers within the safety of a traditional performance.   

Socio-Political Cohesion: The internal structure of the town, characterized by a highly organized system of guilds and brotherhoods (like All Saints, founded in 1301), provided the institutional stability necessary to transmit complex choreography across centuries. The dance became a rite of passage for local men, a symbol of communal strength and identity that was passed from father to son.

Narrative Architecture: Drama and Character Dynamics
The Moreška is structured as a syncretic event—a seamless integration of a written dramatic script and a pyrrhic sword dance. The narrative center is a romantic conflict that many observers compare to the legend of the Trojan War, albeit set within a Mediterranean context of dynastic rivalry. The dialogue is written in an archaic 17th-century Korčulan dialect, which serves to bridge the gap between the medieval roots of the dance and the present-day performance.   

The Core Conflict
The plot centers on the abduction of the Bula, the fiancée of the White King (Osman), by Moro, the son of the Black King (Otmanović). Unlike earlier Spanish versions where the sides were strictly Christian versus Moorish, the Korčulan version shifted in the 19th century to a more allegorical battle between "White" and "Black" armies, or sometimes Turks versus Moors, where the White army—symbolizing good, justice, and the rightful claimant to the maiden—always emerges victorious.

The performance opens with a scene of psychological tension. Moro is seen dragging the Bula in chains, attempting to win her affection. The Bula's response is one of steadfast loyalty to Osman, expressed in blank verse: "My dear, sweet love... all I ask of far more pain for me is your unwanted love than the steel". This dialogue sets the stakes for the ensuing physical combat; the battle is not just over a girl, but over the principles of honor and the sanctity of marriage.   

Choreographic Structure and Symbolic Combat
The transition from the dramatic prologue to the dance is marked by the "Sfida"—the challenge. The Black King dances alone, performing a graceful yet menacing solo in 6/8 rhythm to provoke the White King. This solo serves as a kinetic declaration of war, leading directly into the entry of both armies—the "White" army (in red) and the "Black" army (in black)—who clash in a series of seven ritualized battles known as kolapi (strikes).   

The Seven Kolapa (Figures)
The sequence of the dance is fixed, with each figure escalating in tempo and physical intensity. The clashing of the swords is rhythmic and forceful, often producing sparks as the iron blades strike one another.

The seventh kolap is particularly significant for its tactical imagery. The Black army is eventually surrounded, defending themselves from within a circle that becomes progressively smaller and tighter until they are forced to yield. The final act of the ritual is the Black King surrendering his sword at the White King’s feet, followed by the liberation of the Bula from her chains.   

The Physics of the Sword Strikes
The Moreška is distinguished from other European sword dances by the use of two swords (double sword technique). Historically, until the 1960s, these were of unequal length—a longer sword in the right hand for offense and a shorter blade in the left for defense—mirroring 16th-century martial techniques. Today, the swords are of equal length (approximately 1kg of iron), but the arm movements still preserve the aggressive right and defensive left clashing patterns. The auditory dimension of these strikes is central to the experience; the sound "bam bam bam" echoes throughout the town’s stone streets, signaling the endurance and skill of the moreškanti.   

HGD Sveta Cecilija: A History of Institutional Resilience
The "Hrvatsko glazbeno društvo Sveta Cecilija" (HGD Sveta Cecilija) is the institutional steward of the Moreška, providing the musical and organizational infrastructure necessary for its preservation. Founded in 1883, the society is the oldest civic singing society in Southern Croatia and has played a pivotal role in maintaining Croatian national identity during periods of foreign rule.   

Founding and the Austro-Hungarian Period (1883–1918)
The founding of the society occurred during the Croatian National Revival, a time when cultural organizations were the primary means of asserting local autonomy against the Austro-Hungarian Empire. The society’s original statutes articulated a "noble and lofty purpose": to "ennoble the hearts of the local youth" by nurturing Croatian folk song and language, and to defend "people's sacrosanct principles". During this period, the Moreška was performed as a highlight of local festivities, reinforcing the town's distinct Slavic-Mediterranean character.   

The Interwar and Post-WWII Eras (1918–1990)
Between 1918 and 1939, the Moreška was often performed under the auspices of the "Gymnastic Society of Korčula" (Sokol), but the core practitioners remained linked to the musical and social networks of the Sveta Cecilija members. The Second World War brought a severe interruption; the society ceased its activities, and many of the essential materials for the Moreška—costumes, iron swords, and musical scores—were lost or destroyed in the bombing and ground combat.   

In 1944, as the region was being liberated, a group of dedicated individuals led by the town barber Ivo Lozica and the teacher Bozo Jerirevic worked to revive the dance. They trained a troop of teenagers (aged 12 to 17) from scratch to ensure the tradition did not die with the older generation. During the socialist Yugoslav period, the Moreška was integrated into the "Worker's Cultural Artistic Society" (KUD), though it continued to represent the identity of Korčula on the international stage, including performances at the 1947 World Youth Festival in Prague.   

Modern Era and the 1991 Revival
Following Croatia's declaration of independence in 1991, the HGD Sveta Cecilija was officially re-established. This revival allowed the society to return to its original name and mission. Today, the society comprises approximately 80 members and operates three distinct sections:   

The Moreška Troop: Responsible for the sword dance and dramatic performance.   

The Wind Orchestra: Providing the musical accompaniment for the dance and performing separate concert repertoires.   

Klapa Sveta Cecilija: A male vocal ensemble performing traditional a cappella Dalmatian songs, often serving as the opening act for the Moreška.

Artistic Components: Costuming, Music, and Training
The Moreška is an intricate aesthetic production where the visual and auditory elements are as strictly codified as the choreography.

Evolution of Costume Design
The costumes of the moreškanti are unique within Croatian folklore, featuring a "Roman-Oriental" style that was first described by travelers in the early 19th century. Research suggests these outfits are closely related to 17th-century theatrical costumes used in Italian court ballets, specifically in the Duchy of Savoy, where Roman features were used to denote nobility and military strength.   

The moreškant's attire typically includes:

Suknja (Skirt): A full-length pleated skirt, with a shorter underskirt (sotana) to provide structural volume.   

Bluza (Shirt): The Black army notably wears shirts with long black sleeves.   

Pojas (Belt) and Rubac (Handkerchief): Essential decorative and functional accessories.   

Aline: White fabric cuffs protruding from the shoulders, derived from Roman military spalline.   

A significant stylistic debate occurred in 1947 and 1951, when folklorist Zvonimir Ljevaković attempted to "Muslimize" the costumes by introducing dimije (full pants) and turbans. However, the local performers rejected these changes, eventually returning to the traditional skirted costume that has been the hallmark of the Korčulan Moreška since the late 19th century.   

The Musical Score of Krsto Odak
While the Moreška was historically accompanied by drums or potentially a solo violin, the modern performance relies on a complex brass band arrangement. In 1937, the renowned Croatian composer Krsto Odak, in collaboration with conductor Josip Svoboda, created a new score that has been used exclusively since 1947. This arrangement is designed to mirror the intensifying drama of the seven kolapa; it begins with lyrical themes for the dialogue and builds toward a high-tempo, percussive finale that underscores the "furious" speed of the sword clashes.   

Physical Training and the "Moreškant" Life
The role of a moreškant is physically demanding and socially prestigious. Participation is traditionally limited to males from Korčulan families, and roles like the kings or the Bula are often passed down through generations. Training is rigorous and focuses on:   

Swordsmanship: Mastering the unique double-sword technique where the right hand strikes and the left hand parries.   

Stamina: The dance is an intensive cardiovascular workout, requiring the dancers to maintain high-energy movements for up to 30 minutes in modern abbreviated versions.   

Timing: Dancers must strike with enough precision to avoid injury while ensuring the swords clash hard enough to satisfy the aesthetic requirements of the "war dance".   

The town’s sentiment toward the dance is captured in local sayings such as: "You are not a male if you cannot dance Moreška". This cultural expectation ensures that young boys begin learning the steps in elementary school, viewing their eventual induction into the troop as a vital marker of adulthood and civic pride.   

Cultural Significance and Global Recognition
In the 21st century, the Moreška has transcended its origins as a local ritual to become a symbol of Croatian intangible heritage and a significant global tourist attraction.

The Feast of St. Theodore and the Festival Model
Historically, the Moreška was performed only once a year on July 29th, the Feast of St. Theodore, the town’s patron saint. This was a "lavish presentation" that could last up to two hours. With the growth of the tourism industry in the late 20th century, the performance schedule was expanded to twice weekly (Mondays and Thursdays) during the summer season to accommodate visitors.   

In 1997, the "Sword Dance Festival" was initiated to bring together all sword dance groups on the island, including the Kumpanjija and Moštra from the villages of Žrnovo, Blato, and Vela Luka. This festival, organized by the Korčula Tourist Board and artistic director Dr. Goran Oreb, highlights the island’s status as a "unique treasure chest" of European martial heritage.   

Protected Intangible Heritage
The Moreška is officially recognized as a "protected cultural good" of the Republic of Croatia. This status, granted by the Ministry of Culture, ensures that the dance is safeguarded from commercial dilution and that its traditional elements—such as the double-sword technique and the local dialect dialogue—are preserved in their authentic forms. The HGD Sveta Cecilija, as the primary institutional guardian, remains central to this mission, ensuring that every clash of the swords on the Ljetno Kino stage is a continuation of a 400-year-old dialogue between the past and the present.   

Synthesis of Findings
The Moreška of Korčula is a remarkable survival of a pan-European Mediterranean tradition that has been uniquely localized and institutionalized. Its endurance can be attributed to the deep-seated pride of Korčulan families and the strategic institutional support provided by HGD Sveta Cecilija. As a performance, it remains a "living monument" to the tumultuous history of the Mediterranean, where the stylized struggle between "Black" and "White" serves not to celebrate conflict, but to ritualize the triumph of virtue and the enduring spirit of a community that has successfully defended its identity through centuries of geopolitical change. Through the combination of Krsto Odak’s stirring music, the archival "Roman-Oriental" costumes, and the visceral impact of iron on iron, the Moreška remains an inseparable part of the Korčulan soul, bridging the medieval martial training of a coastal garrison with the cultural aspirations of a modern sovereign state.
