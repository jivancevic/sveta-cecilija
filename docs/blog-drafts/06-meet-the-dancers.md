# Behind the Scenes: Meet the Moreška Dancers

When you watch the Moreška, you see two armies, fourteen flashing swords and a story of love and combat. What you don't see is everything behind it: the months of training, the family lines, and the quiet pride of a community that has handed this dance from one generation to the next for centuries. Meet the **moreškanti** — the people who keep Korčula's sword dance alive.

## Not actors — Korčulans

The first thing to understand is that the Moreška isn't performed by a touring cast. It's performed by the people of Korčula. The dance has traditionally been the domain of local men from Korčulan families, and it's woven so deeply into the town's identity that there's an old local saying: *"You are not a male if you cannot dance Moreška."*

That sense of belonging starts young. Boys here begin learning the steps in **elementary school**, and stepping into the troupe is seen as a marker of adulthood and civic pride. Roles — including the kings and the Bula — are often passed down through generations, from father to son. When you watch a performance, you may well be watching someone do exactly what their father and grandfather did on the same stage.

## What it takes to be a moreškant

The Moreška looks thrilling because it genuinely is demanding. The training focuses on three things:

- **Swordsmanship.** Each dancer wields *two* iron blades — the right hand strikes, the left parries — in the double-sword technique that sets the Moreška apart from every other European sword dance. The swords are real, weighing about a kilogram each.
- **Stamina.** The dance is an intense cardiovascular workout. Dancers sustain high-energy combat for the full sequence of seven escalating battles — physically grueling, especially in the summer heat.
- **Timing and precision.** This is the part that separates a moreškant from someone simply swinging a sword. The blades have to clash *hard* — hard enough to spark and to satisfy the aesthetics of a real war dance — while striking precisely enough that no one gets hurt. That balance comes from months of dedicated practice — roughly ten months to a year and a half to become stage-ready.

It's why the *bam, bam, bam* of the swords sounds so convincing. It isn't choreography mimed at a safe distance; it's controlled, full-contact iron, performed by people who have trained hard for it and grown up with the tradition.

## The people behind the dance — past and present

The Moreška has survived because individuals chose to carry it. The names that saved it are remembered in Korčula:

- **Ivo Lozica**, the town barber, led the post-war revival of 1944 and is remembered as an "indefatigable spirit."
- **Božo Jeričević**, a schoolteacher, trained that first post-war troop of teenagers — aged just 12 to 17 — from scratch, after the war had scattered the costumes, swords and scores.
- **Krsto Odak** and conductor **Josip Svoboda** gave the dance the brass-band score, written in 1937, that still drives every performance.

Today the tradition is carried by a society of around **80 members**, organised into the **Moreška Troop**, the [Wind Orchestra](/sections/wind-orchestra), and [Klapa Sveta Cecilija](/sections/klapa). The dancers you see on stage are the current link in a chain that runs unbroken back through all of them.

## Carrying it forward

Every clash of iron on the Summer Cinema stage is the latest line in a story that's been told for centuries. The moreškanti aren't re-creating history for an audience — they *are* the history, still living, still training, still passing the swords to the next generation of Korčula's sons.
