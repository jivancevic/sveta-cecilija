# The History of Moreška: 400 Years of Sword Dance

The Moreška you can watch in Korčula tonight is the last of its kind. Sword dances like it once spanned the Mediterranean — from Spain to Sicily, from Venice to the Greek islands. Today, only Korčula's survives intact. This is the story of how a medieval battle ritual travelled across an entire sea and found its final home on one Croatian island.

## A dance born from conquest

The Moreška is, at its root, a "dance of conquest" — a stylised retelling of the medieval struggle between Christian Europe and the Islamic world. It belongs to the wider Mediterranean tradition of *Moros y Cristianos* ("Moors and Christians") mock battles. The earliest recorded performance took place in **Lérida, Spain, in 1150**, commemorating the Christian capture of Aragon and staging a mock combat between two armies.

From there the form spread along the trade routes of the Mediterranean. It reached the Adriatic by the 16th century — though some scholars trace its influence as far back as a sword dance reported in **Trogir in 1273**.

## How it reached Korčula

The Moreška arrived in Korčula by sea. Trade routes linked the island to Sicily, Southern Italy and Spain, and from **1420 the town spent nearly four centuries as a Venetian outpost** — a status that funnelled Italian and Spanish cultural forms into local life.

By the mid-17th century the dance was woven into the fabric of the town. The first unambiguous written record of a Moreška performance in Korčula is a **journal entry dated 7 March 1666**, describing a carnival event. Elsewhere on the coast the tradition was already in retreat: a famous staged battle had been performed in **Split in September 1571** to celebrate the victory over the Ottomans at Lepanto, but as the threat of invasion receded, these dances disappeared from town after town. Korčula became the **final bastion** of the authentic war-dance form.

## Why did it survive only here?

Historians and ethnochoreologists have long asked why Korčula, of all places, held on. There is no single answer — but three explanations stand out:

- **Martial preparation.** For a small island garrison frequently targeted by pirates and Ottoman raiders, the dance doubled as a "keep-fit class" and sword practice, keeping local men physically capable with heavy iron blades.
- **A "hidden transcript" of resistance.** Scholars such as Max Harris and Lada Čale Feldman argue the dance let Korčulans express a coded defiance of their foreign rulers — the public story was Christians defeating Moors, but the ritual combat allowed a safer, metaphorical resistance against Venetian or other authority.
- **Social cohesion.** Korčula's tightly organised system of guilds and brotherhoods — like the All Saints brotherhood, founded as far back as 1301 — gave the town the institutional stability to pass complex choreography from one generation to the next. The dance became a rite of passage, handed from father to son.

## A king, a costume, a king's witness

By the 19th century the Moreška had acquired the form we recognise today, with its "White" and "Black" armies and its distinctive **"Roman-Oriental" costumes** — first described by travellers in the early 1800s and likely descended from 17th-century Italian court-ballet costume. We even have a royal eyewitness: in **1838 the Saxon King Frederick Augustus II witnessed a performance**, leaving behind some of the earliest descriptions of the costumes.

## 1883: a society to guard the dance

The turning point for the modern Moreška came in **1883**, during the Croatian National Revival, when local citizens founded the Korčula Singing Society St. Cecily — today's **HGD Sveta Cecilija**. Its statutes set out a "noble and lofty purpose": to "ennoble the hearts of the local youth" through Croatian folk song and language. The society became the institutional home of the Moreška, and remains the oldest civic singing society in Southern Croatia. You can read more about the organisation on our [about page](/about).

A second milestone followed in **1937**, when the renowned Croatian composer **Krsto Odak**, working with conductor **Josip Svoboda**, wrote the definitive brass-band score — seven movements building from lyrical themes to a furious finale. That arrangement has been used at every performance since 1947, and it is the music you still hear today.

## War, ashes, and revival

The 20th century nearly broke the tradition twice.

The **Second World War** silenced the society. Costumes, iron swords and musical scores were lost or destroyed in the fighting. But in **1944, as the region was being liberated**, the town barber **Ivo Lozica** — remembered as an "indefatigable spirit" — and the schoolteacher **Božo Jeričević** trained an entirely new troop of teenage boys, aged 12 to 17, from scratch. Costumes were improvised; the coordinator Bernard Maroević even helped procure fabric from church altars to clothe the dancers. The tradition did not die.

Through the socialist Yugoslav decades the dance continued under different organisational banners, even representing Korčula abroad — including at the **1947 World Youth Festival in Prague**. Then, after Croatia's declaration of independence, **HGD Sveta Cecilija was officially re-established in 1991** under its original name and mission.

## The Moreška today

The society now numbers around 80 members and runs three sections: the **Moreška Troop**, the **[Wind Orchestra](/sections/wind-orchestra)**, and **[Klapa Sveta Cecilija](/sections/klapa)**. Once performed only once a year on **29 July, the Feast of St. Theodore** — a lavish presentation that could run two hours — the Moreška now plays through the summer season so visitors can experience it. In 1997 Korčula even launched a Sword Dance Festival, gathering the island's related traditions in one place.

The Moreška is now a **protected cultural good of the Republic of Croatia**. Nearly nine centuries after that first mock battle in Spain, the same drama still plays out on a stone stage in Korčula — two armies, fourteen iron swords, and a community that refused to let its dance die.
